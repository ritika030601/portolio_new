01. Water Behaviour Research

A behavioural study on why users reach for a new product when a perfectly good one is already in front of them. The research focuses on decision architecture, visibility, and the small design choices that make reuse feel like the obvious move.

01 / Context

Why this study matters

In offices, homes, and shared spaces, people make the same unconscious choice every day: open something new when an existing option is right there. This pattern is not about carelessness - it is about how our brains make quick decisions under uncertainty. As a UX researcher, I wanted to understand that mechanism because the same decision logic plays out in every interface: users default to the familiar, the clear, and the low-risk option. Design that ignores this loses people at the moment of choice.

This research was not focused on a specific product. It was focused on a behaviour pattern - and the design principles that emerge from understanding it are applicable across products, services, and interfaces.

What I found was surprising: the problem is rarely about freshness or cleanliness. It is about perceived state. When users cannot instantly tell whether something is ready to use, they skip it. They choose new not because new is better, but because new is safe.

02 / Research Approach

How I studied the behaviour

I combined three methods to get a complete picture: passive observation to watch behaviour naturally, timed decision tasks to measure speed and confidence, and follow-up interviews to understand the reasoning behind each choice.

Observed 50+ natural decision moments in shared spaces - offices, kitchens, waiting areas - without interfering with normal behaviour.
Ran timed choice tasks where participants had to pick between a labelled existing option and an unlabelled new one. I measured both speed and perceived confidence.
Conducted follow-up interviews focused on the emotional side of the decision: what made the existing option feel uncertain, and what would have made it feel safe to use.

04 / Behavioural Patterns

What I kept seeing

Four patterns showed up consistently across all participants and contexts. These were not outliers - they were the norm.

Sealed Illusion: Users perceived sealed or wrapped products as categorically cleaner, regardless of actual condition. Packaging signals freshness even when freshness is not in question.
State Ambiguity: When an existing option lacked clear visual signals about its condition, users defaulted to skipping it. They did not investigate - they just moved on.
Effort Calculus: The decision to assess an existing option was treated like a small cost. If that cost was non-zero, users chose the zero-effort alternative: something new.
Validation Gap: Users wanted permission to reuse. A simple label, date stamp, or trust cue was enough to overcome the default bias toward new. Without it, the default won every time.

05 / Design Implications

What this means for UX

These patterns are not limited to physical products. The same logic applies to every interface where users choose between an existing state and a new one.

Make the state of existing options immediately legible. If users cannot read the state at a glance, they will ignore it.
Use validation cues - timestamps, status indicators, progress markers - to reduce the perceived risk of engaging with something that already exists.
Position the existing or recommended option first, with the highest visual weight. Do not make users hunt for the low-friction path.
Reduce cognitive load at the moment of choice. Every assessment step you ask users to take is a step toward "choose new." Design decisions should happen before the user arrives, not at the moment of arrival.
