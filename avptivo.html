Fitness and Habit UX

03. Avptivo

Avptivo is a fitness app designed around one belief: people don't quit workouts because they lack motivation, they quit because the app gives them too much to decide and too little structure to lean on. This case study walks through the research, the structural decisions, and the reasoning behind the screens - not just the screens.

02 / Context

Why people stop showing up

Most fitness apps are actually three separate apps stitched together - a workout tracker, a meal logger, and a progress dashboard. Users switch between them, lose context, and eventually stop using all of them.

Five specific problems came out of early research and shaped everything that followed: a lack of a "central hub" left users disorganised about their health goals; fixed workout durations ignored that most people only have 15-20 free minutes; charts and graphs were too technical for the average user to read at a glance; users felt isolated without any social or accountability layer; and vegetarian or vegan users specifically lacked diverse, high-protein meal plans.

None of these are UI problems. They're structural ones - which is why the response couldn't just be "nicer screens."

03 / Research

What users actually said

I interviewed four people across different fitness contexts - a college student, a working professional, a "fit customizer" focused on diet, and a working student - and mapped each through empathy maps before forming any opinion on the solution.

College Student · Biggest mental hurdle
"I want a feature to overcome the initial dread - something like guided warm-ups or beginner challenges to ease into workouts."
Working Professional · Support needed when drained
"I need a flexible workout schedule that adapts to an unpredictable routine, and motivational nudges when I'm feeling drained."
Fit Customizer · Essential customization
"I require extensive dietary customization within the app - specifically robust options for vegetarian meal planning."
Working Student · Ideal tracking system
"I want a simplified, time-saving tracking system with minimal manual input for food logging."
The insight All four had different lives, but the same underlying ask: remove the decision-making, not just the friction. Nobody asked for more features - they asked for fewer choices to make at the moment they were already tired.

04 / Strategy

Reframing the problem before designing the answer

Benchmarking against NTC, Cult, Fitbit, and Strava showed that most competitors compete on feature count - gamified challenges, social feeds, wearable sync - but none of them solved the actual drop-off moment: deciding what to do next. That gap became the brief.

How Might We turn complex biometrics into a simple, intuitive 360° body visual?
How Might We create micro-workouts that keep users consistent even with just 15 minutes?
How Might We use AI to auto-log nutrition from a single photo or quick description?
How Might We build a frictionless daily Metric Hub that encourages effortless progress tracking?

05 / Information Architecture

One product map, four root sections

Before any screen was drawn, the whole app was structured as a flat, four-branch map. This is what separates a UI decision from a UX one - every screen that came later had to justify its place inside this structure.

06 / User Journey

Mapping the four stages of habit drop-off

Mapping the journey from first open to long-term retention surfaced where motivation actually fails - and it isn't where most fitness apps focus their effort.

Stage What the user does Feeling Where Avptivo had to improve
Onboarding & Setup Signs up, enters goals, connects devices Excited Easy setup, clear privacy
Activity & Workout Selection Browses fitness or diet content Excited → Indecisive AI recommendations, less hunting
Tracking & Execution Picks workout, tracks routine Motivated → Frustrated Video demos, real-time feedback, less manual input
Retention & Community Views progress, joins challenges Unmotivated → Happy Mindful nudges, rewards, social challenges

07 / Wireframes → Hi-Fi

From rough boxes to the final flow

Every screen went through a low-fidelity pass first - laid out purely for structure and hierarchy, with no visual design decisions made yet. The hi-fi screens only followed once the structure held up on its own.

Low-Fi Wireframe

Final Hi-Fi Screen

What changed and why: the wireframe pass started with a tab-style metrics list on Home. Testing made it clear users skimmed past blocks of numbers, so the hi-fi version replaced them with a single "next action" card and pushed detailed metrics one tap away into Track - reflecting the IA decision above, not just a visual refresh.

08 / Design Decisions

Why each key screen looks the way it does

Home Screen

I designed the home screen to always surface one clear next action instead of a menu of options - because research showed users dropped off most often when the app asked them to decide what to do, not when it told them.

Post-Workout Meal Suggestion

I tied meal suggestions to the workout just completed instead of a generic daily plan, because the Fit Customizer persona specifically needed nutrition to feel connected to effort, not like a separate admin task.

Progress & Streak View

I placed streaks and weekly totals inline, immediately after a completed session, rather than behind a separate tab - because the research showed progress only motivated users when it was visible in context, right after the action that earned it.

If this shipped, the metric I'd watch first is 7-day workout retention - whether the daily-loop home screen actually keeps people returning past the first week, which is where most fitness apps lose users.
