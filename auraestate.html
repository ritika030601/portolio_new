Dual-Mode Real Estate

04. Aura Estate

Aura Estate is a refined mobile real estate platform built around a hard constraint: investors and first-time buyers are looking at the same listings for completely different reasons, and forcing them through one generic flow serves neither well. This case study covers the research behind that split, and the structural choices that came out of it.

01 / Context

When one flow serves no one well

Aura Estate is a refined mobile real estate platform that simplifies property discovery through two tailored user journeys. It balances investor-focused financial insight with an aspirational, lifestyle-driven experience for first-time buyers - designed with a warm, minimalist palette of deep oak, sand, and cream linen as a deliberate alternative to cluttered, traditional real estate interfaces.

Benchmarking Aura Estate against MagicBricks and NoBroker made the gap clear: both competitors default to a "one size fits all" interface. Neither gives investors real ROI tooling, and neither gives first-time buyers a guided, low-friction path - cognitive load on both platforms came out "extremely high" to "moderate" at best.

The question wasn't whether to build two apps or one app - it was whether a single shared entry point could set the right context immediately, then branch into two genuinely different experiences without feeling disjointed.

02 / Research

Two personas, two completely different jobs to be done

Research centred on building out two full personas through an affinity diagram - clustering needs into Financial Performance, Portfolio Management, and Market Intelligence for investors, versus Lifestyle Discovery, Emotional Connection, and Educational Guidance for first-time buyers - then validating each against a real bio and a clear set of pain points.

Marcus Sterling
35 · Senior Portfolio Manager

A seasoned real estate professional managing high-value assets. He prioritises market data, financial forecasting, and operational efficiency over lifestyle aesthetics.

Pain Points

  • Analysis paralysis
  • Data lag
  • Administrative friction

Needs

  • Predictive insights
  • Real-time returns
  • Secure document storage
Maya Stephen
28 · Creative Lead / Designer

A young professional transitioning from renting to owning. She wants an emotional connection to a space, but is intimidated by the complex financial and legal steps involved.

Pain Points

  • Goal uncertainty
  • Poor discoverability
  • Buyer confusion

Needs

  • Clean cost breakdowns
  • Lifestyle-based search
  • A guided journey
The insight Both personas needed the platform to earn their trust fast - Marcus through verifiable numbers, Maya through clarity and reassurance. The trust mechanism had to be different for each, even though the underlying need was the same.

05 / Wireframes → Hi-Fi

From rough boxes to the final flow

Both modes were wireframed independently before any colour or imagery was added, to make sure the structural decisions held up before the "warm minimalist" visual language was layered on top.

Low-Fi Wireframe

Final Hi-Fi Screen

What changed and why: the early investor wireframes led with a property photo at the top of the dashboard, matching the buyer layout. In testing, this read as noise to the investor persona - so the hi-fi version moved photography to a secondary position and pushed yield, NOI, and portfolio performance to the top of the screen instead.

06 / Design Decisions

Why each key screen looks the way it does

Entry / Mode Selection

I chose a single, prominent two-option toggle at entry instead of a multi-step questionnaire, because both personas dropped off in benchmarked competitors when onboarding felt like a form rather than a single decision.

Investor Dashboard

I surfaced market analytics and projected returns as the primary content instead of property photography, because Marcus's research explicitly ranked lifestyle imagery as noise that got in the way of his decision.

Buyer Property View

I led the buyer view with photography and neighbourhood story content, with financial data present but not pushed forward, because Maya needed an emotional read on a space before she was ready to engage with numbers.

08 / Outcomes

What I'd measure if this shipped

For Investor mode, the first metric I'd track is time-to-first-vault-document-upload - a proxy for whether Marcus actually trusts the platform enough to move his real paperwork into it.
For Buyer mode, I'd track completion rate of the guided move-in journey - whether Maya makes it from "saved a favourite" through to "contacted an agent" without abandoning midway, which is exactly where the research showed buyer confusion peaked.
Across both modes, mode-switch rate would tell me whether the shared brand and entry point are actually working - ideally, switching feels like a preference, not a different product.
