01 / Context

The problem with food rescue

Every evening, restaurants throw away food that was prepared but not sold. Meanwhile, NGOs that feed communities are often one step behind — they find out about donations too late, can't arrange pickup in time, and the food is already gone. The infrastructure for redistribution exists: restaurants, drivers, and NGOs are all willing to participate. The coordination is what breaks down.

MingleMeal is a platform designed to fix that coordination problem. But unlike a typical logistics app with one user type, this one needed to serve three completely different users simultaneously — each with different goals, different constraints, and different definitions of what "working" means.

02 / Research

What I found when I listened

I started by talking to each user group separately, before I touched a single wireframe. What became clear immediately was that each group had a completely different mental model of "the problem" — and any design that tried to solve all three from the same perspective would fail all three.

Research 01

Restaurant interviews (Donors)

I spoke with restaurant managers and kitchen staff about their current process for handling surplus food. The dominant theme was time pressure. Restaurants operate on tight margins and tight schedules. They wanted to donate — but they couldn't hold food indefinitely waiting for someone to collect it. If no one confirmed pickup within a window, the food would be discarded rather than risk keeping it past service hours.

Research 02

NGO interviews (Receivers)

NGO coordinators described a recurring failure point: they'd receive a call about available food when it was already too late to arrange collection. Availability wasn't the issue — speed was. They needed earlier notice, a way to claim food before it became urgent, and clear confirmation that a driver was on the way. Without those signals, coordinating volunteers was chaotic.

Research 03

Driver interviews (Connectors)

Volunteer drivers described a different set of frustrations: wrong pickup addresses, contacts who didn't answer, food that had already been discarded by the time they arrived. The handoff moments — pickup from restaurant, delivery to NGO — were the points of highest failure. Drivers needed clear, verified information and a way to confirm handoffs unambiguously at both ends.

Research synthesis

All three users were failing because of the same root cause: no shared, real-time picture of what was happening. The restaurant didn't know if anyone was coming. The NGO didn't know if a driver was confirmed. The driver didn't know if the food was still there. Solving MingleMeal meant creating that shared visibility — and doing it fast enough that food wouldn't spoil while users waited for information.

04 / Who Uses This

Three users, one system

The biggest structural challenge of MingleMeal is that it can't be designed for one user type. Each role has distinct goals, distinct friction points, and distinct mental models. Here's how I defined each one.

User Type 01

The Restaurant — Donor

Goal: Post surplus food quickly and get confirmation it will be collected — without keeping staff waiting.

Constraint: Time. Food has a hard expiry window post-service. Anything that adds steps or delays loses them.

Core pain: Uncertainty about whether anyone is coming — and no way to know without calling.
User Type 02

The NGO — Receiver

Goal: Maximise the quantity and quality of food received for redistribution.

Constraint: Volunteer coordination. They can't dispatch someone without confirmation that the food and a driver are both secured.

Core pain: Finding out about donations too late to act on them effectively.
User Type 03

The Driver — Connector

Goal: Complete pickup and delivery efficiently, on a schedule that fits around other commitments.

Constraint: Reliability of information. Wrong details waste their time and erode their willingness to volunteer again.

Core pain: Arriving at a pickup point to find the food is already gone, with no prior notification.

Mapping this flow revealed the two highest-risk handoff points: the pickup moment (where food could be discarded before the driver arrived) and the delivery confirmation (where NGOs had no way to formally close the loop). Both became the basis for the QR verification system.

06 / Design Decisions

Why I made the choices I made

Every key screen in MingleMeal is the result of a specific decision. Here are the four that shaped the product most.

Decision 01 · Onboarding

I chose role-based onboarding — users select Restaurant, NGO, or Driver on first launch — rather than a shared universal dashboard.

The information architecture for each user type is completely different. A restaurant needs to post and monitor. An NGO needs to browse, claim, and coordinate. A driver needs a task list and map. Combining them into one dashboard created visual noise that slowed all three users down in early testing. Separating them at onboarding meant each user only ever sees what's relevant to their role — and switching is possible if needed (some NGO workers also volunteer as drivers).

Decision 02 · Verification

I chose QR code scanning at both the pickup and delivery points, rather than a simple "mark as collected" button.

Research showed that food handoffs were failing silently: drivers would mark food as collected, but the restaurant and NGO weren't informed in real time and couldn't verify the handoff had actually happened. A simple button could be tapped without the driver being on-site. QR scanning creates an unambiguous verification moment — the driver must physically be at the location with the restaurant's code. This reduced failed handoffs and gave all three parties confidence in the status.

Decision 03 · Information hierarchy

I made time remaining the dominant piece of information on every food listing — larger and more prominent than food type or quantity.

A food listing expiring in 45 minutes triggers completely different behaviour than one expiring in 3 hours. For restaurants, it changes whether they wait or post urgently. For NGOs, it changes whether they dispatch a driver now or plan for later. For drivers, it changes route priority. After testing several hierarchy arrangements, time-first reduced misclicks on expired listings and reduced the number of cases where drivers were assigned to food that was no longer available.

Decision 04 · Driver navigation

I chose a persistent bottom navigation bar for the driver interface rather than a hamburger menu or sidebar.

Drivers use the app while on the move — sometimes on a scooter, often with one hand. A hamburger menu requires two deliberate taps to switch between the map, task list, and delivery confirmation screen. A bottom nav keeps all three primary actions within thumb reach at all times. Accessibility and single-hand usability were not secondary considerations here — they were the entire brief for the driver role.

08 / What Success Looks Like

Measuring what matters

MingleMeal is a concept project, but the research is grounded in real user needs. If this were shipped, here's how I'd measure whether it was working:

>90%
Pickup confirmation rate
Percentage of food listings that result in a confirmed, QR-verified pickup. The baseline from research: many donations currently go uncollected due to coordination failure.
↓ Claim time
Time-to-claim
How quickly an NGO claims a food listing after it's posted. Faster claiming means more food gets collected before the pickup window closes.
0 silent fails
Handoff accuracy
Number of cases where a driver is assigned to food that is no longer available. QR verification and real-time status should reduce this to zero.
What I learned from this

MingleMeal taught me that multi-sided platform design requires you to do research with all stakeholders before you design for any of them. The decisions I made that had the most impact — QR verification, time-first hierarchy, role-based onboarding — all came directly from research, not instinct. Without the interviews, I would have built a cleaner-looking app that failed at every real-world handoff.
